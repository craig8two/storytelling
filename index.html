RECONSIDERING
MALNUTRITION
IN THE SAHEL

The image of a malnourished child in the Sahel is no longer just a dangerously thin child who isn’t getting enough to eat. Today, the vast majority of children in the region are affected by at least 3 different forms of malnutrition – wasting, stunting and hidden hunger.

WASTING

Wasting is an extreme form of malnutrition which results in a child being too thin for his/her height. Given the impact this condition has on the immune system, it carries an immediate increased risk of death.

STUNTING

Stunting is a condition where a child is too short for their age. It reflects chronic undernutrition during the most critical periods of growth and development in early life. Stunted children are more susceptible to infection and do not fully develop physically or cognitively.

HIDDEN HUNGER

Hidden hunger occurs when a child doesn’t get enough essential vitamins and minerals which are necessary for healthy growth and development. Vitamin A, iodine and iron deficiencies are the most common.

* Terminology: For consistency, we have used the term ‘wasting’ rather than ‘acute malnutrition’, as wasting is often not an acute event.

How we’re going to do it

Nutrition is a lifelong issue.

The effects of poor nutrition start in the womb, but continue into adulthood, and cycle across generations. While malnutrition can trap generations of children in a cycle of poverty, good nutrition, particularly in infancy, is the building block for future health and development. A lifecycle approach to nutrition programming ensures that we target nutrition programmes to the key moments in a person’s life.

First 1000 days of life
Micronutrients help support healthy pregnancy, prevent anaemia, promote foetal growth, and ensure that babies are born at a healthy birth weight.

They are crucial to the health and survival of the growing baby – but also to the health and well-being of girls and women themselves. Micronutrient supplementation can be particularly important in countries where gender inequality denies women access to nutritious foods and appropriate care during pregnancy.

Childhood
Throughout childhood, good nutrition continues to play an important role in keeping children strong, healthy and free of disease.

Working with partners to improve water and sanitation is critical because diarrhoeal disease in childhood, linked to unsafe drinking water, makes it impossible for a child to absorb sufficient nutrients even when appropriate foods are being consumed. UNICEF also provides nutritional support through health and community services.

Adolescence
Phenomenal growth occurs in adolescence – second only to that in the first year of life – creating increased demand for energy and nutrients.

Given that many adolescent girls and women have nutrient deficits, UNICEF supports programmes that provide micronutrients such as iron and folate during this critical time.

Pregnancy
Micronutrients help support healthy pregnancy, prevent anaemia, promote foetal growth, and ensure that babies are born at a healthy birth weight.

They are crucial to the health and survival of the growing baby – but also to the health and well-being of girls and women themselves. Micronutrient supplementation can be particularly important in countries where gender inequality denies women access to nutritious foods and appropriate care during pregnancy.

NUTRITION
LIFECYCLE
UNICEF’s Nutrition programming is focused on prevention first,
and providing early and quality treatment as soon as it’s required.

The programming is grounded in a systems-strengthening approach to improve nutrition outcomes for children, adolescents and women. This approach activates 5 key systems (food, health, water and sanitation, education and social protection) that have the potential to impact on nutrition-specific interventions at scale, leveraging them to deliver nutrition results that go beyond their own sectoral objectives.

The nutrition crisis, exacerbated by COVID-19, is a call to accelerate efforts and an opportunity to re-imagine the way we do business in nutrition.
